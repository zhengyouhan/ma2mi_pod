Individual vehicle trajectories are essential for safety analysis, driving behavior studies, and training autonomous vehicle systems. Yet collecting such data is expensive, typically requiring instrumented vehicles, drone footage, or high-resolution camera networks. In contrast, macroscopic measurements from loop detectors (flow, density, speed) are ubiquitous but aggregate away individual vehicle information. This raises a valuable question: can we reconstruct vehicle trajectories from macroscopic observations alone?

As one of the most extensively studied topics, trajectory reconstruction and generation has been investigated for decades. Recent breakthroughs in learning-based methods dominate the benchmarks and show an encouraging future. However, most of these methods require a tremendous amount of data for fidelity. Besides, due to the inherent drawbacks of machine learning frameworks, learning-based trajectory generation methods usually offer limited interpretability and lack generality under different or extreme traffic scenarios. Another promising direction is differentiable simulation, which addresses both limitations: it leverages explicit physical models (providing interpretability) while requiring no microscopic training data (only macroscopic observations to match). By computing gradients of any output with respect to inputs, differentiable simulation enables efficient optimization through the physics itself. This paradigm has transformed adjacent fields: in robotics, where rigid-body engines enable learning locomotion policies~\cite{degrave2019,freeman2021}; in graphics, where differentiable rendering recovers 3D scenes from 2D images~\cite{li2018differentiable,nimier2019mitsuba}; and in fluids, where differentiable solvers accelerate design optimization~\cite{um2020solver,holl2020phiflow}.

In transportation, however, differentiable simulation remains largely unexplored. The reason is structural: traffic models are traditionally discrete. Cellular automata use integer cell occupancy; event-driven simulations trigger on lane changes; car-following models switch abruptly between regimes via hard conditionals. These discontinuities break gradient flow---small parameter changes crossing thresholds produce discrete jumps with undefined gradients.

This paper presents a training-free approach that brings differentiable simulation to traffic. We formulate trajectory reconstruction as an inverse problem: find behavioral parameters such that a physics-based simulator, when rolled forward, produces trajectories whose aggregated measurements match observations. Unlike learning-based methods requiring large trajectory datasets~\cite{alahi2016}, our approach leverages only car-following physics---no microscopic ground truth is needed.

We demonstrate feasibility by reformulating the Intelligent Driver Model (IDM) with soft approximations that preserve gradient flow while closely matching original dynamics. Combined with Gaussian kernel aggregation that differentiably maps trajectories to detector measurements, this enables end-to-end optimization from macroscopic observations back to microscopic parameters. The micro-macro inverse problem is inherently ill-posed: many distinct parameter configurations can produce similar aggregate measurements. We address this through Graph Laplacian regularization on the leader-follower topology, which enforces parameter smoothness between consecutive vehicles and encodes the structural prior that drivers in close-following relationships exhibit similar behavior.

This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A training-free framework for reconstructing microscopic trajectories from macroscopic observations, combining differentiable IDM dynamics with Gaussian kernel aggregation operators;
    \item Graph Laplacian regularization on the leader-follower topology that exploits the car-following structure to control ill-posedness, enforcing parameter smoothness between consecutive vehicles;
    \item Experimental validation on NGSIM achieving 6\% density and 10\% velocity reconstruction error without any microscopic training data.
\end{enumerate}
